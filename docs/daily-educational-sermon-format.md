# Daily Hebrew Educational Sermon Format

Status: Permanent production contract
Approved by: Ace
Applies to: Every newly created morning Hebrew Bible study lesson and its audio script

## Core promise

Every lesson must be genius-level in thought and fifth-grade in language. It should feel like a warm educational sermon, not a classroom worksheet, encyclopedia entry, database dump, or list of references.

Each lesson must be built around one memorable central truth that comes honestly from the verse. Every Hebrew explanation, Strong's word story, cross reference, illustration, application, and Jesus connection must serve that one central truth.

## Sermon movement

1. Open with a real human struggle, question, picture, or everyday moment.
2. Read the KJV verse reverently.
3. State one clear central truth in one memorable sentence.
4. Read the natural Hebrew and transliteration.
5. Explain the Hebrew slowly and visually.
6. Tell the story of the important Hebrew roots and Strong's words.
7. Show how the idea develops across Scripture through carefully chosen cross references.
8. Apply the verse to normal life and to AceOS when appropriate.
9. Show Jesus carefully and honestly. Label direct prophecy, pattern, echo, and theological connection accurately.
10. End with an invitation, prayer, and final natural Hebrew reading.

## Existing app compatibility

Keep the existing audio segment architecture and lesson schema. The sermon movement should flow through the current segments rather than replacing them. The opening, word study, cross references, Did You Know? See Jesus Here section, reflection, and prayer should sound like connected movements of one message.

## Teaching standard

Think deeply and explain simply. Use accurate Hebrew, grammar, theology, literary structure, historical context, and whole-Bible connections. Use short sentences, concrete pictures, and everyday comparisons. Define technical terms immediately. The listener should feel, “I understand something deep,” not, “I survived a lecture.”

## Strong's word stories

Do not read bare reference lists. For each important Strong's entry:

- Verify an approximate Hebrew Old Testament occurrence count and name the source.
- Explain the normal range of meaning.
- Describe the kinds of scenes where the word appears.
- Trace two to four representative passages as a connected story.
- Explain what stays consistent and what changes.
- Distinguish the root or lemma from the exact form when needed.
- Explain what the word carries into the present verse.

Never invent an occurrence count. State when counts are approximate or sources differ.

## Voice

The voice is Michael: Ace's warm, funny COO and personal Bible teacher. Use brief affectionate humor, vivid comparisons, and an occasional lovable Michael Scott moment. Never joke disrespectfully about Scripture, Hebrew, Jewish people, our Father, Jesus, or prayer. Let serious spiritual moments breathe.

## Audio writing

Write spoken text as natural speech. Use connecting sentences and smooth transitions. Avoid stacked labels, abbreviations, slash-heavy notes, semicolon chains, and rapid citation lists. Normal teaching should use natural sentence rhythm. Prayer and reverent reading should be slightly slower. Slow Hebrew should flow by phrase, not by clipped syllables.

## Required quality gate

Before audio generation, confirm that the lesson has:

- one clear central truth
- a strong opening hook
- connected sermon movement
- accurate Hebrew and grammar
- narrative Strong's teaching
- meaningful biblical cross references
- practical application
- a careful Jesus connection
- a reverent closing prayer

Revise before generating audio when the lesson reads like disconnected notes or reference material.

## Definition of done

A lesson is not complete until the written study passes the quality gate, the full audio track is ready and published, every segment is ready, all required metadata exists, and every audio object is verified in the `hebrew-media` storage bucket.
